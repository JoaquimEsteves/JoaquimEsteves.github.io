KPMG Luxembourg

Through CBTW : from 05/2022 - 08/2022

Context:

KPMG's HR have a problem.

Every year they need to calculate raises and bonuses for each of the employees. Back when the branch was a few hundred employees this was all done on a massive excel spreadsheet; but suddenly the Luxembourgish branch grew to a gargantuan 2000 employees and clearly some sort of app needed to be made.

They then made a grave mistake - they hired consultants.

The consultants delivered a terrible mess. A C# back-end full of business rules that contradict each other sprayed to-and-fro. A "classic" jQuery + Bootstrap Front-End with yet more business rules that contradict the former ones.

Nobody internal to the company wanted to work on this project, the problem was that the code was bad, but not bad enough that they could redo the whole thing; it had been trudging along, for 5 years, collecting patches left and right to attempt to fill the leaks. However, it was clearly an important, yet, sensitive project.

So what could KPMG's HR team do? They hired more consultants (Me).

(I hope the reader will understand that the context section was written with sarcasm and exaggeration in mind. The codebase was quite rocky, but the team was solid and we ended up doing a good job).

Wut I did:

I was the Front-End expert; for the majority of the project I worked alone, and by the end I was training my replacement.

The interesting thing about being the sole Front-End expert is that you end up acting as a sort of lightning rod for all of the problems with the application, as non-technical people can't quite explain why something is wrong, only that it is wrong. So we encountered success within the project not due to technical skill, but due to clarity and patience.

The previous consultants were not very meticulous about their linting or the code formatting; so setting up a proper pre-commit hook to ensure code-quality was a tremendous boon. Setting up CI/CD was also crucial, as before the code was built and pushed through good old ssh ( cowboy sys-admin style ).

We also got rid of any cleverness, no more fa√ßades or dependency injections, we wanted stupid boring code with lots of comments.

Dealing with confusing business rules was the great issue, the solution was lots of calls with the relevant stake-holders.

It sounds tremendously obvious; but when the code would say something, the manual another and the stake-holder a third thing it was faster to just call and hash it out with a small meeting. Remote work tools were a tremendous boon in this; we rarely spent more than 15 minutes on a call.

I was quite lucky to have a tremendous team, and a very patient HR liaison. Sadly everyone was quite cross with us in the end; as the bonuses and raises for that year were terrible.

Tech Stack

Typescript, React
